Formula 1 Strategy Prediction and Visualization¶

The Problem¶

"Speed. I am speed. One winner, 42 losers." Lightning McQueen’s iconic words capture the essence of racing and thirst for glory. Just like in Cars, every Formula 1 race crowns only one winner. But unlike the animated world of Pixar, much of the battle in Formula 1 is fought off the track where cutting edge CFD simulations, intricate data analysis, and precise calculations shape the path to victory. One of the most important components of having a successful race weekend is developing a proper strategy. The strategy for a race weekend involves creating a set of optimal pit windows and planning out for every possible scenario. To accomplish this task it is integral to build an idea of the general race pace of the car. A simulated race pace can help inform the strategic operations team about who they will end up competing with on track and when to tell the driver to push or conserve. It also allows them to determine when to pit the car to maximize the performance of each set of tires. I aim to reveal the relationship between qualifying time and average lap time to develop a linear regression model so I can predict what the average lap time could look like. With a reliable prediction of what the average lap time could be it would become much easier to form a strategy.¶

The Data¶

To find a relationship between qualifying time and lap time I first need to source a reliable dataset with features that include at least those two aspects. Luckily I found a great dataset on Kaggle with the basic features I needed and additional categories such as every single lap time of every race or all pit stops ever taken. I'll include a link to the dataset at the end along with my other references if you want to check it out yourself. While the dataset had plenty of data including CSV tables with information on races, pitstops, constructors, drivers, lap times, and results for my purposes I will only be using the laptimes, pitstops, races, and circuits tables.¶

The Pre-processing¶

The dataset encompasses Formula 1 from 1950 to 2024 and while that range may be useful for other tasks it will just add extra noise and dilute the relationship between qualifying pace and average lap time. I decided to limit the data from 2014-2021 as that will encompass the entire turbo-hybrid era so it will allow for a large amount of data to look through while keeping it in a generally similar set of engine and aero regulations. There are 3 qualifying sessions that we will end up using with drivers getting knocked out each round depending on their position in qualifying.¶

    def time_to_ms(time_str):
        if pd.isna(time_str):
            return None
        try:
            minutes, seconds = time_str.split(':')
            return (float(minutes) * 60 + float(seconds)) * 1000
        except:
            return None

The Story¶

Congratulations. We've now determined many of the aspects needed to form a competitive race strategy. As we saw in the plot there was a clear positive linear relationship between qualifying 1 time and the average lap times. With the model that we developed, we can use our driver's Q1 time to get a reasonable approximation of what their average lap time during the race will be. It was determined that Q2 and Q3 times don't necessarily have a stronger relationship with average lap time so we don't have to worry if our driver did not make it out of Q1. There is a proven correlation between our drivers performance in qualifying is to what their race pace will be so we know that whoever qualified around us will be who we are competing against during the race. We also now have a general idea of what our pit stop time will look like depending on the circuit. For example, if we are racing at the Sochi autodrome we know that our pit stop will be generally over 30 seconds so it is integral that we try to pit under safety car or try our best to minimize the impact of the pit stop.¶

The Impact¶

While we can now make much stronger predictions on possible race pace and plan out race strategy to a much higher level there are many impacts that I would be remiss to leave out. Unfortunately, while data-driven insights provide a plan for the most optimal race strategy one must consider the possible effect it has on the spirit of the sport. It is relatively common to see cars let faster cars pass them by so they don't have to waste tire grip and lose time fighting against the other car that they know they have no hope of beating. This however can take away great moments in the races whether it's a rookie in a backmarker fighting against a veteran in a top car or just two midfield cars fighting it out. You also risk taking out some of the driver's feedback by listening to just the data. By finding the most optimal time to pit you don't pay attention to what the driver feels in the car and risk losing some great moments like William's Alex Albon staying on the same set of tires for the entire race. There are also a few integral aspects that this dataset is missing out on. We do not take into account tire wear or tire compound which can be a huge factor in race strategy and the weather is also not considered which leaves out a huge feature as sometimes it can completely change the face of a race. While many aspects could be affected by paying so much attention to the data to form your strategy Formula 1 is ultimately a data-driven sport and performance gains are meant to be taken by any possible opportunity.¶
